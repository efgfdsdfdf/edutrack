Chapter 1: The Bennet Family

It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife. However little known the feelings or views of such a man may be on his first entering a neighbourhood, this truth is so well fixed in the minds of the surrounding families, that he is considered as the rightful property of some one or other of their daughters.

"My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"

Mr. Bennet replied that he had not.

"But it is," returned she; "for Mrs. Long has just been here, and she told me all about it."

Mr. Bennet made no answer.

"Do not you want to know who has taken it?" cried his wife impatiently.

"You want to tell me, and I have no objection to hearing it."

This was invitation enough.

"Why, my dear, you must know, Mrs. Long says that Netherfield is taken by a young man of large fortune from the north of England; that he came down on Monday in a chaise and four to see the place, and was so much delighted with it that he agreed with Mr. Morris immediately; that he is to take possession before Michaelmas, and some of his servants are to be in the house by the end of next week."

"What is his name?"

"Bingley."

"Is he married or single?"

"Oh! single, my dear, to be sure! A single man of large fortune; four or five thousand a year. What a fine thing for our girls!"

"How so? How can it affect them?"

"My dear Mr. Bennet," replied his wife, "how can you be so tiresome! You must know that I am thinking of his marrying one of them."

"Is that his design in settling here?"

"Design! nonsense, how can you talk so! But it is very likely that he may fall in love with one of them, and therefore you must visit him as soon as he comes."

"I see no occasion for that. You and the girls may go, or you may send them by themselves, which perhaps will be still better, for as you are as handsome as any of them, Mr. Bingley might like you the best of the party."

"My dear, you flatter me. I certainly have had my share of beauty, but I do not pretend to be anything extraordinary now. When a woman has five grown-up daughters, she ought to give over thinking of her own beauty."

"In such cases, a woman has not often much beauty to think of."

"But, my dear, you must indeed go and see Mr. Bingley when he comes into the neighbourhood."

"It is more than I engage for, I assure you."

"But consider your daughters. Only think what an establishment it would be for one of them. Sir William and Lady Lucas are determined to go, merely on that account, for in general, you know, they visit no newcomers. Indeed you must go, for it will be impossible for us to visit him if you do not."

"You are over-scrupulous, surely. I dare say Mr. Bingley will be very glad to see you; and I will send a few lines by you to assure him of my hearty consent to his marrying whichever he chooses of the girls; though I must throw in a good word for my little Lizzy."

"I desire you will do no such thing. Lizzy is not a bit better than the others; and I am sure she is not half so handsome as Jane, nor half so good-humoured as Lydia. But you are always giving her the preference."

"They have none of them much to recommend them," replied he; "they are all silly and ignorant like other girls; but Lizzy has something more of quickness than her sisters."

"Mr. Bennet, how can you abuse your own children in such a way? You take delight in vexing me. You have no compassion on my poor nerves."

"You mistake me, my dear. I have a high respect for your nerves. They are my old friends. I have heard you mention them with consideration these twenty years at least."

"Ah, you do not know what I suffer."

"But I hope you will get over it, and live to see many young men of four thousand a year come into the neighbourhood."

"It will be no use to us, if twenty such should come, since you will not visit them."

"Depend upon it, my dear, that when there are twenty, I will visit them all."

Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice, that the experience of three-and-twenty years had been insufficient to make his wife understand his character. Her mind was less difficult to develop. She was a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper. When she was discontented, she fancied herself nervous. The business of her life was to get her daughters married; its solace was visiting and news.

The arrival of Mr. Bingley at Netherfield Park would soon set in motion events that would change the lives of the Bennet family forever.

Chapter 2: The Meryton Assembly

The Meryton assembly ball brought together the neighborhood's most eligible families, setting the stage for first impressions that would prove both lasting and misleading.

Mr. Bingley had not been of age two years when he was tempted by an accidental recommendation to look at Netherfield House. He did look at it, and into it for half-an-hour—was pleased with the situation and the principal rooms, satisfied with what the owner said in its praise, and took it immediately.

Between him and Darcy there was a very steady friendship, in spite of great opposition of character. Bingley was endeared to Darcy by the easiness, openness, and ductility of his temper, though no disposition could offer a greater contrast to his own, and though with his own he never appeared dissatisfied. On the strength of Darcy's regard, Bingley had the firmest reliance, and of his judgment the highest opinion. In understanding, Darcy was the superior. Bingley was by no means deficient, but Darcy was clever. He was at the same time haughty, reserved, and fastidious, and his manners, though well-bred, were not inviting. In that respect his friend had greatly the advantage. Bingley was sure of being liked wherever he appeared, Darcy was continually giving offense.

The manner in which they spoke of the Meryton assembly was sufficiently characteristic. Bingley had never met with more pleasant people or prettier girls in his life; everybody had been most kind and attentive to him; there had been no formality, no stiffness; he had soon felt acquainted with all the room; and, as to Miss Bennet, he could not conceive an angel more beautiful. Darcy, on the contrary, had seen a collection of people in whom there was little beauty and no fashion, for none of whom he had felt the smallest interest, and from none received either attention or pleasure. Miss Bennet he acknowledged to be pretty, but she smiled too much.

Mrs. Hurst and her sister allowed it to be so—but still they admired her and liked her, and pronounced her to be a sweet girl, and one whom they would not object to know more of. Miss Bennet was therefore established as a sweet girl, and their brother felt authorized by such commendation to think of her as he chose.

Elizabeth Bennet had been obliged, by the scarcity of gentlemen, to sit down for two dances; and during part of that time, Mr. Darcy had been standing near enough for her to overhear a conversation between him and Mr. Bingley, who came from the dance for a few minutes to press his friend to join it.

"Come, Darcy," said he, "I must have you dance. I hate to see you standing about by yourself in this stupid manner. You had much better dance."

"I certainly shall not. You know how I detest it, unless I am particularly acquainted with my partner. At such an assembly as this, it would be insupportable. Your sisters are engaged, and there is not another woman in the room whom it would not be a punishment to me to stand up with."

"I would not be so fastidious as you are," cried Mr. Bingley, "for a kingdom! Upon my honour, I never met with so many pleasant girls in my life as I have this evening; and there are several of them you see uncommonly pretty."

"You are dancing with the only handsome girl in the room," said Mr. Darcy, looking at the eldest Miss Bennet.

"Oh! she is the most beautiful creature I ever beheld! But there is one of her sisters sitting down just behind you, who is very pretty, and I dare say very agreeable. Do let me ask my partner to introduce you."

"Which do you mean?" and turning round he looked for a moment at Elizabeth, till catching her eye, he withdrew his own and coldly said: "She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me; I am in no humour at present to give consequence to young ladies who are slighted by other men. You had better return to your partner and enjoy her smiles, for you are wasting your time with me."

Mr. Bingley followed his advice. Mr. Darcy walked off; and Elizabeth remained with no very cordial feelings toward him. She told the story, however, with great spirit among her friends; for she had a lively, playful disposition, which delighted in anything ridiculous.

The evening altogether passed off pleasantly to the whole family. Mrs. Bennet had seen her eldest daughter much admired by the Netherfield party. Mr. Bingley had danced with her twice, and she had been distinguished by his sisters. Jane was as much gratified by this as her mother could be, though in a quieter way. Elizabeth felt Jane's pleasure. Mary had heard herself mentioned to Miss Bingley as the most accomplished girl in the neighbourhood; and Catherine and Lydia had been fortunate enough to be never without partners, which was all that they had yet learnt to care for at a ball.

Elizabeth's first impression of Mr. Darcy was firmly set: he was the proudest, most disagreeable man in the world.

Chapter 3: Mr. Collins' Proposal

The arrival of Mr. Collins brought with it an unexpected proposal that would test Elizabeth's resolve and principles.

Mr. Collins was not a sensible man, and the deficiency of nature had been but little assisted by education or society. A fortunate chance had recommended him to Lady Catherine de Bourgh when the living of Hunsford was vacant; and the respect which he felt for her high rank, and his veneration for her as his patroness, mingling with a very good opinion of himself, of his authority as a clergyman, and his rights as a rector, made him altogether a mixture of pride and obsequiousness, self-importance and humility.

Having now a good house and a very sufficient income, he intended to marry; and in seeking a reconciliation with the Longbourn family he had a wife in view, as he meant to choose one of the daughters, if he found them as handsome and amiable as they were represented by common report. This was his plan of amends—of atonement—for inheriting their father's estate; and he thought it an excellent one, full of eligibility and suitableness, and excessively generous and disinterested on his own part.

His plan did not vary on seeing them. Miss Bennet's lovely face confirmed his views, and established all his strictest notions of what was due to seniority; and for the first evening she was his settled choice. The next morning, however, made an alteration; for in a quarter of an hour's conversation with Mrs. Bennet before breakfast, a conversation beginning with his parsonage-house, and leading naturally to the avowal of his hopes that a mistress might be found for it at Longbourn, produced from her such a broad hint of her youngest daughter's being likely to be very soon engaged, as made his caution determine him to fix on Elizabeth.

The business of actually making the offer to Elizabeth now occupied his thoughts. He found her alone in the breakfast-parlor.

"Believe me, my dear Miss Elizabeth, that your modesty, so far from doing you any disservice, rather adds to your other perfections. You would have been less amiable in my eyes had there not been this little unwillingness; but allow me to assure you that I have your respected mother's permission for this address. You can hardly doubt the purport of my discourse, however your natural delicacy may lead you to dissemble; my attentions have been too marked to be mistaken. Almost as soon as I entered the house I singled you out as the companion of my future life."

Elizabeth could not but look surprised.

"You are too hasty, sir," she cried. "You forget that I have made no answer. Let me do it without further loss of time. Accept my thanks for the compliment you are paying me. I am very sensible of the honour of your proposals, but it is impossible for me to do otherwise than to decline them."

"I am not now to learn," replied Mr. Collins, with a formal wave of the hand, "that it is usual with young ladies to reject the addresses of the man whom they secretly mean to accept, when he first applies for their favour; and that sometimes the refusal is repeated a second or even a third time. I am therefore by no means discouraged by what you have just said, and shall hope to lead you to the altar ere long."

"Upon my word, sir," cried Elizabeth, "your hope is a rather extraordinary one after my declaration. I do assure you that I am not one of those young ladies (if such young ladies there are) who are so daring as to risk their happiness on the chance of being asked a second time. I am perfectly serious in my refusal. You could not make me happy, and I am convinced that I am the last woman in the world who could make you so."

Mr. Collins continued to insist on his prospects and connections, emphasizing his relationship with Lady Catherine de Bourgh and the advantages such a marriage would bring to the Bennet family, given the entailment of Longbourn.

Elizabeth remained firm in her refusal, growing increasingly frustrated with his inability to accept her answer. "I am very sensible of the honour of your proposals, but it is impossible for me to do otherwise than decline them."

"I know it to be the established custom of your sex to reject a man on the first application, and perhaps you have even now said as much to encourage my suit as would be consistent with the true delicacy of the female character."

"Really, Mr. Collins," cried Elizabeth with some warmth, "you puzzle me exceedingly. If what I have hitherto said can appear to you in the form of encouragement, I know not how to express my refusal in such a way as to convince you of its being one."

Elizabeth's firmness did eventually convince Mr. Collins that her refusal was sincere, though he attributed it to the "usual practice of elegant females." He departed with his pride wounded but intact, convinced that Elizabeth's refusal was merely part of the customary feminine coquetry.

Elizabeth had preserved her independence, but her refusal would have consequences she could not yet foresee.

Chapter 4: Netherfield Park

Jane's illness during her visit to Netherfield Park brought Elizabeth into closer contact with Mr. Darcy, revealing unexpected dimensions to his character.

When Jane fell ill during her visit to Netherfield Park, Elizabeth's concern for her sister overcame any hesitation about imposing on the Bingley household. She walked three miles through muddy fields to reach Netherfield, arriving with a face glowing with the warmth of exercise, her hair blown by the wind, and her petticoat six inches deep in mud.

Her appearance created a great deal of surprise at Netherfield. That she should have walked three miles so early in the day, in such dirty weather, and by herself, was almost incredible to Mrs. Hurst and Miss Bingley; and Elizabeth was convinced that they held her in contempt for it. She was received, however, very politely by them; and in their brother's manners there was something better than politeness; there was good humour and kindness.

Mr. Darcy said very little. He was employed in admiring the animation of her eyes, which, though bright, made no considerable addition to the exercise that had given a glow to her face. To Mr. Darcy it was welcome intelligence—Elizabeth had been at Netherfield long enough. She attracted him more than he liked—and Miss Bingley was uncivil to her, and more teasing than usual to himself.

During her stay, Elizabeth had several opportunities to observe Mr. Darcy more closely. She noticed that he frequently watched her, and though his manner remained reserved, there were moments when she caught an expression in his eyes that she could not quite interpret.

One evening, as they were all together in the drawing-room, Miss Bingley attempted to engage Mr. Darcy in conversation by inviting him to join her in walking about the room.

"You had much better stay where you are," said Elizabeth, who was observing them from her seat near the fire.

Darcy looked at her with a slight smile and said, "You are willfully to misunderstand me, Miss Bennet. A walk about the room, I believe, would be very refreshing after sitting so long in one attitude."

"I am not to be intimidated into anything so wholly unreasonable," she replied. "Your retrospections must be so totally void of reproach, that the contentment arising from them is not of philosophy, but what is much better, of ignorance."

"There is, I believe, in every disposition a tendency to some particular evil—a natural defect, which not even the best education can overcome."

"And your defect is to hate everybody."

"And yours," he replied with a smile, "is willfully to misunderstand them."

Elizabeth found herself enjoying these exchanges more than she cared to admit. There was an intelligence in his conversation that challenged her own, and though she still thought him proud and disagreeable, she could not deny the sharpness of his mind.

As Jane's health improved and the time for their departure approached, Elizabeth found herself with mixed feelings. She was eager to return home, yet strangely reluctant to leave Netherfield. The complexity of Mr. Darcy's character had begun to intrigue her, though she would not yet admit it to herself.

On their final evening, Elizabeth played the pianoforte for the company. Mr. Darcy moved to stand near her, and though he said little, his attention was fixed upon her. Miss Bingley, noticing his absorption, made several pointed remarks about country manners and the disadvantages of a limited education, but Elizabeth bore them with good humor.

When Elizabeth and Jane finally departed the next morning, Mr. Darcy watched from the window as their carriage disappeared down the drive. His feelings were in conflict—relief at the restoration of peace to his friend's household, and an unexpected sense of loss at Elizabeth's absence.

Mr. Darcy found himself increasingly captivated by the very woman he had once deemed merely "tolerable."

Chapter 5: The Letter

Mr. Darcy's unexpected proposal and the letter that followed would force Elizabeth to confront her own prejudices and misunderstandings.

Mr. Darcy's proposal came as a complete surprise to Elizabeth. They were walking in the lane near the Parsonage when he appeared unexpectedly. After some awkward conversation, he began with evident agitation:

"In vain I have struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

Elizabeth's astonishment was beyond expression. She stared, colored, doubted, and was silent. This he considered sufficient encouragement; and the avowal of all that he felt, and had long felt for her, immediately followed.

He spoke well, but there were feelings besides those of the heart to be detailed. His sense of her inferiority—of its being a degradation—of the family obstacles which judgment had always opposed to inclination, were dwelt on with a warmth which seemed due to the consequence he was wounding, but was very unlikely to recommend his suit.

In spite of her deeply-rooted dislike, she could not be insensible to the compliment of such a man's affection, and though her intentions did not vary for an instant, she was at first sorry for the pain he was to receive; till, roused to resentment by his subsequent language, she lost all compassion in anger.

He concluded with representing to her the strength of that attachment which, in spite of all his endeavors, he had found impossible to conquer; and with expressing his hope that it would now be rewarded by her acceptance of his hand.

As he said this, she could easily see that he had no doubt of a favorable answer. He spoke of apprehension and anxiety, but his countenance expressed real security.

When he ceased, the color rose into her cheeks, and she said:

"In such cases as this, it is, I believe, the established mode to express a sense of obligation for the sentiments avowed, however unequally they may be returned. It is natural that obligation should be felt, and if I could feel gratitude, I would now thank you. But I cannot—I have never desired your good opinion, and you have certainly bestowed it most unwillingly. I am sorry to have occasioned pain to anyone. It has been most unconsciously done, however, and I hope will be of short duration. The feelings which, you tell me, have long prevented the acknowledgment of your regard, can have little difficulty in overcoming it after this explanation."

Mr. Darcy, who was leaning against the mantelpiece with his eyes fixed on her face, seemed to catch her words with no less resentment than surprise. His complexion became pale with anger, and the disturbance of his mind was visible in every feature.

After a silence of several minutes, he came towards her in an agitated manner, and said, "And this is all the reply which I am to have the honor of expecting! I might, perhaps, wish to be informed why, with so little endeavor at civility, I am thus rejected. But it is of small importance."

"I might as well inquire," replied she, "why with so evident a design of offending and insulting me, you chose to tell me that you liked me against your will, against your reason, and even against your character? Was not this some excuse for incivility, if I was uncivil? But I have other provocations. You know I have. Had not my feelings decided against you—had they been indifferent, or had they even been favorable, do you think that any consideration would tempt me to accept the man who has been the means of ruining, perhaps forever, the happiness of a most beloved sister?"

The next day, Mr. Darcy delivered a letter to Elizabeth at the Parsonage. In it, he addressed her accusations with startling frankness.

"You may possibly wonder why all this was not told you last night; but I was not then master enough of myself to know what could or ought to be revealed."

He acknowledged his role in separating Bingley from Jane, but defended his actions by explaining that he had genuinely believed Jane indifferent to his friend. More shockingly, he revealed the truth about Mr. Wickham—his attempted elopement with Darcy's young sister Georgiana, motivated by her fortune of thirty thousand pounds.

Elizabeth read the letter with a mixture of shock, disbelief, and dawning realization. The evidence Mr. Darcy presented was detailed and convincing, supported by dates and circumstances that aligned perfectly with what she knew of Wickham's history.

"I will only add, God bless you," the letter concluded. "Fitzwilliam Darcy."

For hours, Elizabeth walked through the park, reading and rereading the letter. With each reading, her prejudices began to crumble. She saw how completely she had misjudged both Darcy and Wickham, how her vanity had been flattered by Wickham's attentions and wounded by Darcy's reserve.

"How despicably I have acted!" she cried. "I, who have prided myself on my discernment! I, who have valued myself on my abilities! Till this moment I never knew myself."

Elizabeth's entire understanding of the world had been overturned in a single morning.

Chapter 6: Pemberley

Elizabeth's visit to Pemberley would reveal Mr. Darcy in a new light, challenging all her previous assumptions about his character.

When Elizabeth toured Pemberley with her aunt and uncle Gardiner, she was struck by the beauty and elegance of the estate, but even more by what she learned of its master from the housekeeper, Mrs. Reynolds.

"He is the best landlord, and the best master," said Mrs. Reynolds, "that ever lived. There is not one of his tenants or servants but will give him a good name. Some people call him proud; but I am sure I never saw anything of it. To my fancy, it is only because he does not rattle away like other young men."

Elizabeth listened with increasing astonishment as the housekeeper continued to praise Mr. Darcy's character, his generosity to his sister, his kindness to the poor, and his fairness in all his dealings.

"I have never had a cross word from him in my life," Mrs. Reynolds declared, "and I have known him ever since he was four years old."

This picture of Mr. Darcy was so different from the man Elizabeth thought she knew that she could scarcely believe they were the same person. As she walked through the beautiful grounds of Pemberley, she found herself imagining what it might be like to be mistress of such an estate—and then blushed at her own presumption.

It was at this moment, when her thoughts were most occupied with Mr. Darcy, that he himself appeared. He had returned to Pemberley a day earlier than expected.

The meeting was awkward for both of them. Elizabeth was acutely conscious of her previous behavior toward him, while Mr. Darcy seemed determined to show that his feelings had changed.

To Elizabeth's astonishment, he was perfectly civil—more than civil, he was attentive and kind. He spoke to her aunt and uncle with respect, invited them to fish in his streams, and even asked permission to introduce his sister to them.

Georgiana Darcy proved to be a shy, sweet-tempered girl of sixteen, very unlike the proud, disagreeable creature Elizabeth had imagined. The brother and sister were clearly devoted to each other, and Mr. Darcy's tenderness toward Georgiana revealed a side of his character Elizabeth had never seen before.

Over the next few days, Mr. Darcy called frequently at the inn where the Gardiners were staying, and often joined them in their walks. His manner toward Elizabeth was so markedly different from what it had been in Hertfordshire that she could hardly believe he was the same man.

One afternoon, as they walked together along the river, Mr. Darcy said, "I have been a selfish being all my life, in practice, though not in principle. As a child I was taught what was right, but I was not taught to correct my temper. I was given good principles, but left to follow them in pride and conceit."

Elizabeth listened with growing emotion. "Your character was decided by your parents?"

"Yes—and such. I was spoilt by my parents, who, though good themselves, allowed, encouraged, almost taught me to be selfish and overbearing—to care for none beyond my own family circle, to think meanly of all the rest of the world, to wish at least to think meanly of their sense and worth compared with my own."

Elizabeth found herself deeply moved by this confession. She realized that Mr. Darcy had undergone a significant transformation—and that she was largely responsible for it.

As their time in Derbyshire drew to a close, Elizabeth found herself wishing it could continue. The man she had once despised had become someone she respected—and perhaps something more.

Just as Elizabeth's feelings toward Mr. Darcy were undergoing a profound change, devastating news from Longbourn would interrupt their growing intimacy.

Chapter 7: Lydia's Elopement

The shocking news of Lydia's elopement with Mr. Wickham threatened to bring disgrace upon the entire Bennet family.

The letter from Jane arrived at the Lambton inn with news that sent Elizabeth into immediate distress. Lydia had eloped with Mr. Wickham, and they had not gone to Scotland to be married as expected, but were missing somewhere in London.

"I am grieved indeed," Jane wrote, "grieved—shocked. But I am willing to hope the best. They may not yet be married. Could he be so unprincipled?"

Elizabeth knew the answer only too well. After Mr. Darcy's revelations about Wickham's character, she had no doubt that he was capable of the worst behavior. The thought that her youngest sister—only sixteen—was in the power of such a man was agony.

Mr. Darcy arrived at the inn shortly after Elizabeth received the letter. He found her in tears, and with little hesitation, she shared the dreadful news with him.

"This is unfortunate indeed," he said after a long silence. "It will be impossible for you to see them married. Is there nothing to be done?"

Elizabeth shook her head hopelessly. "My father is gone to London to try to discover them. But oh! who is to be relied on? How are they even to be supported?"

Mr. Darcy paced the room in obvious agitation. "I who have prided myself on my discernment! I who have valued myself on my abilities! How humbled I am now."

Elizabeth could only look at him in surprise. "If you had behaved in a more gentlemanlike manner," she murmured, not intending him to hear.

But he did hear, and stopped his pacing to look at her. "You are right. I have been selfish and overbearing. But believe me, I am now suffering from the consequences of my own faults."

He left soon after, and Elizabeth returned to Longbourn with her aunt and uncle, her heart heavy with anxiety and shame.

Days passed with no news of Lydia and Wickham. Mr. Bennet returned from London having accomplished nothing. The family lived in a state of constant dread, expecting each day to bring the news that would ruin them all.

Then, unexpectedly, a letter arrived from Mr. Gardiner. He had found the couple, and Wickham had agreed to marry Lydia in exchange for an annual income. The sum mentioned was far more than the Bennets could afford, but Mr. Gardiner wrote that he had taken care of the financial arrangements himself.

Mrs. Bennet was ecstatic. "My dear, dear Lydia!" she cried. "This is delightful indeed! She will be married! I shall see her again! She will be married at sixteen!"

Elizabeth shared none of her mother's joy. She knew that Wickham would never have married Lydia without significant financial incentive, and she suspected that her uncle could not possibly afford the sum mentioned. There was a mystery here that troubled her deeply.

When Lydia and Wickham came to Longbourn after their marriage, Lydia was as thoughtless and vulgar as ever, completely unaware of the disgrace she had brought upon her family. In her usual indiscreet manner, she let slip a piece of information that stunned Elizabeth.

"And I am going to thank Mr. Darcy for being so kind as to find us and make Wickham marry me," she said casually.

Elizabeth stared at her. "Mr. Darcy?"

"Oh yes! He was there at the wedding. I thought you knew. He paid all of Wickham's debts and bought him his commission in the regulars. It must have cost him at least ten thousand pounds!"

Elizabeth was speechless. Mr. Darcy—the man she had refused so vehemently—had saved her family from utter ruin. He had done it quietly, without seeking credit or recognition, enduring the company of the man he despised most in the world.

In that moment, Elizabeth understood the depth of Mr. Darcy's transformation—and the depth of his feelings for her.

Elizabeth now knew the truth about Mr. Darcy's role in saving her family, but had no idea how she would ever repay him—or if she would even have the opportunity.

Chapter 8: The Resolution

As misunderstandings cleared and truths were revealed, Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy found their way to each other at last.

When Mr. Bingley returned to Netherfield Park, accompanied by Mr. Darcy, the neighborhood was all astonishment. Mrs. Bennet immediately began planning how to secure Mr. Bingley for Jane, while Elizabeth found herself in a state of nervous anticipation about seeing Mr. Darcy again.

Their first meeting was formal and restrained. Mr. Darcy was polite but distant, and Elizabeth could not determine whether his feelings toward her had changed. She watched him closely whenever they were in company together, looking for some sign of his former regard.

Mr. Bingley's attentions to Jane were immediately renewed, and it was soon apparent to everyone that his affection had not diminished during his absence. Within a short time, he proposed, and Jane accepted with quiet joy.

Mrs. Bennet was ecstatic. "My dearest Jane! I knew she could not be so beautiful for nothing! Ten thousand a year! Oh, my sweetest Jane, I am so happy!"

Elizabeth shared her sister's happiness, but her own heart was troubled. Mr. Darcy had been present during the celebrations, but had said little to her. She began to fear that his feelings had changed, that her family's behavior during Lydia's crisis had destroyed whatever regard he might have had for her.

Then, one afternoon when Elizabeth was walking alone in the garden, Mr. Darcy appeared. He looked more agitated than she had ever seen him.

"Miss Bennet," he began, "I can no longer struggle against my feelings. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you."

Elizabeth stared at him in astonishment. This was so different from his first proposal—there was no mention of her inferior connections, no expression of conflicted feelings.

"My affections and wishes are unchanged," he continued, "but one word from you will silence me on this subject forever. If, however, your feelings are what they were last April, tell me so at once. My feelings are now, and will forever be, independent of your answer."

Elizabeth found her voice at last. "My feelings have undergone so material a change since the period to which you allude, that I can now receive your gratitude and pleasure in acknowledging them."

The happiness which this reply produced was such as he had probably never felt before. He expressed himself on the occasion as sensibly and as warmly as a man violently in love can be supposed to do.

They walked together for two hours, sharing all that had been in their hearts during their separation. Elizabeth confessed her shame at her former prejudice, and Mr. Darcy acknowledged the justice of her earlier reproofs.

"You taught me a lesson, hard indeed at first, but most advantageous. By you, I was properly humbled. I came to you without a doubt of my reception. You showed me how insufficient were all my pretensions to please a woman worthy of being pleased."

"And I," said Elizabeth, "have long been most heartily ashamed of my behavior. I have been a selfish being all my life, in practice, though not in principle. I was given good understanding, but left to follow it in pride and conceit."

Mr. Darcy smiled at her repetition of his own words. "We have both been improved, I hope. I have learned to think better of myself, and you have learned to think more charitably of others."

When they returned to the house, Elizabeth shared her news with Jane, who received it with as much delight as she had felt in her own engagement.

Mr. Bennet required more convincing. "Lizzy," he said when she told him, "I know your disposition. You could be neither happy nor respectable unless you truly esteemed your husband. Is it really the case that you love Mr. Darcy?"

"I do, Papa. I love him. Indeed, he has no improper pride. He is perfectly amiable."

Mr. Bennet gave his consent, though he could not help adding, "I know you could be neither happy nor respectable unless you truly esteemed your husband—unless you looked up to him as a superior. Your lively talents would place you in the greatest danger in an unequal marriage. I am happy to find that this is not the case with Mr. Darcy."

The double wedding of Jane and Elizabeth was a joyful occasion for the Bennet family. Mrs. Bennet was at last able to see three of her daughters well married, and looked forward with confidence to the eventual establishment of the remaining two.

As for Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy, their marriage was one of true minds and hearts. The lessons they had learned from each other created a foundation of mutual respect and understanding that would sustain them throughout their lives together.

With the marriages of Jane and Elizabeth, the Bennet family's fortunes were secured, and two couples found the happiness that had once seemed so elusive.

Epilogue: Happily Ever After

Years later, the bonds of family and friendship continued to strengthen, proving that true love and understanding can overcome even the deepest prejudices.

In the years that followed, Pemberley became a true home to Elizabeth, who proved to be exactly the mistress the great estate required. Her intelligence, warmth, and genuine interest in others won the respect and affection of tenants and neighbors alike.

Mr. Darcy continued to grow under Elizabeth's influence, becoming more open and sociable while retaining the essential integrity that had always characterized him. The transformation was noted by all who knew him, and was a source of quiet satisfaction to Elizabeth.

Jane and Mr. Bingley settled at an estate within thirty miles of Pemberley, allowing the sisters to maintain the close relationship that had always been so important to them. Their visits were frequent and joyful occasions that brought together two families now united by the deepest bonds of affection.

With time, even Lady Catherine de Bourgh was eventually reconciled to the marriage. Her visits to Pemberley, though still characterized by her usual dictatorial manner, became less frequent occasions of conflict and more opportunities for Elizabeth to demonstrate the grace and intelligence that even Lady Catherine could not help but respect.

Kitty Bennet spent much time with her married sisters, and under their guidance, her character greatly improved. Removed from Lydia's influence and exposed to better company, she became less flighty and more sensible. She eventually made a respectable marriage to a clergyman near Pemberley.

Mary, the only Bennet sister to remain at Longbourn, became her mother's primary companion. She found satisfaction in her books and her music, and occasionally visited her married sisters, though she preferred the quiet routine of her father's house.

As for Lydia and Wickham, their marriage proved to be exactly what might have been expected. Their affection for each other soon sunk into indifference, and their financial circumstances remained precarious despite Mr. Darcy's continued, though discreet, assistance. They moved frequently, always living beyond their means and always expecting others to solve their problems.

Mr. and Mrs. Bennet continued at Longbourn, their lives made more comfortable by the knowledge that their daughters were well settled. Mrs. Bennet remained as nervous and silly as ever, but was now able to boast of her daughters' fine marriages without the anxiety that had previously characterized her matchmaking efforts.

One autumn afternoon, as Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy walked together through the grounds of Pemberley, she turned to him with a smile. "Do you remember what you said to me at the Meryton assembly? That I was merely 'tolerable'?"

Mr. Darcy returned her smile, his eyes warm with affection. "The recollection of what I then said gives me acute pain. I can barely think of it without abhorrence."

"You were not much kinder when you proposed to me the first time," Elizabeth reminded him gently.

"My behavior was unpardonable. I still marvel that you could ever have forgiven me."

Elizabeth took his arm. "We have both changed, and for the better. You have taught me the folly of my prejudices, and I hope I have helped you to overcome yours."

"You have done more than that, my dearest Elizabeth. You have taught me what true happiness is."

They walked on in comfortable silence, the golden autumn leaves swirling around them. The journey that had begun with misunderstanding and prejudice had led them to a love that was deeper and truer than either could have imagined.

At Pemberley, surrounded by family and friends who truly valued them for who they were, Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy had found not just a marriage of convenience or fortune, but a partnership of equals—a union of minds and hearts that would bring them joy for all their days together.
